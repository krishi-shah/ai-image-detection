## 1. CNN-Based Detection of AI-Generated Images

### 1.1 Wang et al. (2020) — CNN-Generated Images Are Surprisingly Easy to Spot...For Now

Wang et al. (2020) trained a ResNet-50 classifier on images generated by ProGAN and tested whether it could detect outputs from 11 other GAN architectures it had never seen during training. The result was surprisingly positive — a single model generalised well across the GAN family.

The key to this generalisation was data augmentation. Adding Gaussian blur and JPEG compression during training stopped the model from overfitting to ProGAN-specific patterns and forced it to learn more general features shared across GANs. This suggests that all GANs leave behind some common statistical traces, likely due to their shared adversarial training process and use of upsampling layers.

However, this study has important limitations for the present project:

- **Only tested on GANs.** The study was conducted before diffusion models (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E) and autoregressive generators (GPT-4o) existed. It remains unknown whether the common "GAN fingerprint" extends to these newer, fundamentally different architectures.
- **Augmentation may not generalise further.** The blur and JPEG augmentation worked because it weakened the high-frequency artifacts GANs produce. Diffusion models already produce smoother outputs naturally, so this augmentation trick may not help a detector bridge the gap to newer models.
- **No calibration analysis.** The study did not examine whether the model's confidence scores were reliable, or whether it could signal uncertainty when encountering images from unseen generators.

### 1.2 Bird and Lotfi (2024) — CIFAKE

Bird and Lotfi (2024) created the CIFAKE dataset — 60,000 real images from CIFAR-10 paired with 60,000 AI-generated counterparts made using Stable Diffusion v1.4. The dataset is balanced, low-resolution (32×32), and designed for straightforward binary classification experiments. This is the primary training dataset used in this project.

Their experiments showed that standard architectures (CNN, DenseNet, ResNet) achieved over 92% accuracy on CIFAKE, with the best exceeding 97%. This confirms that detecting Stable Diffusion outputs is achievable when the test data matches the training distribution.

Their most relevant contribution is the use of **LIME** to explain what the classifier was looking at when making decisions. They found that the classifier focused on different image regions for real vs. AI-generated images, particularly areas with subtle texture inconsistencies.

This project replaces LIME with **Grad-CAM** for explainability, which offers practical improvements:
- Grad-CAM produces cleaner, more spatially coherent heatmaps using a single backward pass through the network.
- LIME requires hundreds of forward passes (perturbing the image repeatedly), making it slow and noisy.
- Grad-CAM shows which feature maps matter most, giving insight into the level of abstraction at which the classifier makes its decision.

### 1.3 Gragnaniello et al. (2021) — Are GAN Generated Images Easy to Detect?

Gragnaniello et al. (2021) challenged the optimistic view from earlier work by showing that cross-GAN generalisation is more fragile than it appears. Their main findings:

1. **Different GANs leave different fingerprints.** ProGAN produces grid-like frequency patterns from progressive upsampling. StyleGAN leaves a different signature from its modulated convolutions. A detector trained on one may not recognise the other.

2. **There is a trade-off between accuracy and transferability.** Detectors that rely on low-level artifacts (frequency anomalies, pixel correlations) score higher within their training distribution but transfer worse to new generators. Detectors that learn higher-level semantic inconsistencies transfer better but are less accurate overall.

3. **Post-processing breaks detectors.** Simple operations like resizing or JPEG compression can remove the low-level artifacts that detectors depend on, causing accuracy to drop even on the same generator family.

For this project, these findings predict that a detector trained on CIFAKE will have learned Stable Diffusion-specific artifacts. It may partially work on StyleGAN if there are shared low-level properties, but should fail more severely on GPT-4o and Midjourney v6, which use different generation processes and apply extensive post-processing.

### 1.4 Corvi et al. (2023) — On the Detection of Synthetic Images Generated by Diffusion Models

Corvi et al. (2023) is the most directly relevant prior work. They tested whether detectors trained on GAN outputs could detect diffusion model outputs and found a clear generalisation gap: GAN-trained classifiers dropped to near-chance accuracy (50–60%) on images from DALL-E 2, Stable Diffusion, and GLIDE. The reverse also held — diffusion-trained detectors performed poorly on GANs.

Their analysis showed why:
- GAN images have distinctive frequency peaks from upsampling operations. Diffusion outputs have much flatter spectra, closer to real photographs, so GAN-trained detectors cannot find the patterns they expect.
- Using t-SNE to visualise intermediate features, they showed that GAN and diffusion images occupy separate regions in feature space — confirming that detectors learn distribution-specific features rather than general forgery features.
- Retraining on mixed data (both GANs and diffusion) improved results but still showed degradation compared to in-distribution testing.

**Gaps that this project addresses:**
- Corvi et al. did not test on the **newest autoregressive generators** (GPT-4o, Midjourney v6), which likely present an even greater detection challenge.
- They did not measure **calibration** — whether the detector was confidently wrong or appropriately uncertain on out-of-distribution inputs.
- They did not use **visual attribution** (like Grad-CAM) to show where the detector looks when it succeeds vs. fails on different generator families.

## 2. Evolution of Generative Models

Each generation of image generators uses a fundamentally different mechanism, which produces different types of artifacts. This progression is key to understanding why detection becomes harder over time.

### 2.1 GANs

GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) generate images through competition between two networks: a generator that creates images from random noise, and a discriminator that tries to tell them apart from real images. Over time, the generator learns to produce increasingly realistic outputs.

ProGAN (Karras et al., 2018) improved quality by training progressively — starting at low resolution and adding detail layers over time. StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) and StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020) further improved realism by using a style-based architecture that gave finer control over image properties.

**Common GAN artifacts:**
- Checkerboard patterns from transposed convolution layers used for upsampling (Odena et al., 2016), visible as regular grid structures in frequency analysis.
- Spectral peaks at specific frequencies tied to the upsampling factor.
- Subtle colour distribution shifts compared to natural images.
- Local structural errors (asymmetric features, distorted backgrounds), though these have reduced with newer architectures.

These shared artifacts explain why Wang et al.'s detector could generalise across the GAN family — they all use similar upsampling operations that leave similar traces.

### 2.2 Diffusion Models

Latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), including Stable Diffusion and DALL-E 2/3 (Ramesh et al., 2022), work completely differently. They start with pure noise and gradually remove it over many steps, guided by a trained denoising network. The final result is decoded from a compressed latent space to a full image using a VAE decoder.

**Why their artifacts differ from GANs:**
- There are no upsampling convolutions, so checkerboard patterns do not occur.
- The iterative denoising naturally smooths out high-frequency noise, producing spectra that look more like real photographs.
- The main potential fingerprint comes from the VAE decoder, which may introduce subtle blurring, but this is less distinctive than GAN artifacts.
- Errors tend to be semantic (wrong number of fingers, distorted text) rather than statistical — and therefore invisible to frequency-based detectors.

### 2.3 Autoregressive and Hybrid Models

The newest generators — GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and Midjourney v6 (Midjourney, 2024) — represent another shift:

- **GPT-4o** generates images as sequences of visual tokens using autoregressive prediction, similar to how it generates text. This produces completely different statistics from both GANs and diffusion models.
- **Midjourney v6** uses a proprietary architecture with extensive fine-tuning and post-processing, refined through human feedback to systematically eliminate detectable artifacts.
- Both models were trained on billions of images and apply multiple refinement stages that suppress any residual statistical signatures.

**The core problem:** Each new generation removes the specific artifacts that previous detectors relied on. GAN detectors learned upsampling patterns → diffusion models removed upsampling. Diffusion detectors learned denoising traces → autoregressive models removed denoising. This creates an escalating gap where older detectors become increasingly useless against newer generators.

## 3. Facial Manipulation Detection: FaceForensics++

Rossler et al. (2019) created FaceForensics++, a benchmark for detecting facial manipulations (face swaps, reenactment). While this project focuses on full image generation rather than face manipulation specifically, FaceForensics++ offers useful methodological lessons:

1. **Compression degrades detection.** Detector accuracy dropped substantially on compressed images, mirroring the challenge this project faces — images shared on social media are always compressed, potentially removing the artifacts detectors rely on.

2. **Deeper architectures perform better.** XceptionNet outperformed shallower networks, suggesting that detecting subtle forgery indicators requires deep feature extraction. This supports this project's choice of EfficientNet-B3, which offers similar depth with better efficiency.

3. **Binary vs. multi-class framing.** FaceForensics++ tested both detecting manipulation (binary) and identifying the method used (multi-class). This project uses binary classification but leverages Grad-CAM to reveal whether the detector implicitly learns generator-specific features.

FaceForensics++ also provides a useful out-of-distribution test: its manipulations use methods (face swapping, warping) completely different from both GANs and diffusion models. Testing the CIFAKE-trained detector on these images would confirm whether it has learned anything general about manipulation or only Stable Diffusion-specific patterns.

## 4. Visual Explainability: Grad-CAM

### 4.1 How It Works

Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017) explains CNN decisions by computing which parts of an image were most important for a particular classification. It works by:

1. Taking the gradient of the predicted class score with respect to the feature maps in a chosen convolutional layer.
2. Averaging these gradients across spatial dimensions to get an importance weight for each feature map.
3. Computing a weighted sum of the feature maps using these weights.
4. Applying ReLU (keeping only positive contributions) to produce a heatmap showing which regions supported the prediction.

The heatmap is upsampled to the original image size and overlaid as a colour map, with warm colours indicating regions the model relied on most.

### 4.2 Why Grad-CAM Over Alternatives

| Method | How It Works | Pros | Cons |
|--------|-------------|------|------|
| LIME (Bird & Lotfi, 2024) | Perturbs image regions, observes output changes | Works with any model | Slow, noisy, results vary between runs |
| Grad-CAM (this project) | Uses gradients from a single backward pass | Fast, deterministic, spatially coherent | Resolution limited by feature map size |
| SHAP | Game-theory based attribution | Strong theoretical basis | Too slow for images |
| Attention maps | Uses built-in attention weights | No extra computation | Only works for attention-based architectures |

Grad-CAM suits this project because: it works directly with EfficientNet-B3's convolutional layers; it gives the same result every time (important for fair comparison across generators); and its slightly coarse resolution is actually helpful — it shows broad spatial regions rather than individual pixels, making it clear whether the detector is looking at meaningful content (faces, objects) or background texture.

### 4.3 How This Project Uses Grad-CAM

The key contribution is using Grad-CAM **comparatively** across different generator families:

- **On CIFAKE images (Stable Diffusion):** Where does the detector look when it correctly identifies AI-generated images? Expected: texture regions with diffusion-specific smoothness.
- **On StyleGAN images:** Does it attend to the same or different regions? Does it succeed using the same features?
- **On GPT-4o / Midjourney v6 images:** When the detector incorrectly labels these as real, what regions drove that wrong decision? This reveals what newer generators have successfully fixed to evade detection.

No prior work has performed this kind of comparative Grad-CAM analysis across multiple generator families. It provides the visual explanation for why the generalisation gap exists.

## 5. Confidence Calibration

### 5.1 The Problem

Guo et al. (2017) showed that modern deep networks are poorly calibrated — their confidence scores do not match their actual accuracy. A model that says it is 95% confident may only be correct 75% of the time. This matters for any real-world detection system because users need to know how much to trust each prediction.

The main causes are:
- Large model capacity leads to overconfident predictions on the training distribution.
- Training objectives (cross-entropy loss) do not penalise overconfidence once the prediction is already correct.
- Batch normalisation and regularisation techniques interact to sharpen output distributions.

### 5.2 Measuring Calibration: ECE

Expected Calibration Error (ECE) measures how well confidence matches accuracy. It works by:

1. Grouping all predictions into bins based on their confidence level (e.g., 0–10%, 10–20%, ..., 90–100%).
2. For each bin, comparing the average confidence to the actual accuracy.
3. Taking the weighted average of these differences.

ECE = 0 means perfect calibration (confidence always equals accuracy). Higher values mean worse calibration.

### 5.3 Fixing Calibration: Temperature Scaling

Temperature scaling is a simple fix applied after training. It introduces a single parameter T that softens (T > 1) or sharpens (T < 1) the model's confidence distribution:

calibrated_confidence = softmax(logits / T)

T is learned on a validation set by minimising the calibration error. Despite its simplicity (just one parameter), Guo et al. showed it works as well as or better than more complex methods.

### 5.4 Why Calibration Matters for This Project

Calibration is especially important here because the detector will encounter images from generators it was never trained on. A well-calibrated model should:

1. **Express uncertainty on unfamiliar inputs.** When shown GPT-4o images that differ from its training data, it should produce confidence scores near 0.5 (uncertain) rather than making high-confidence errors.

2. **Reveal the danger level of failures.** A detector that drops from 95% to 70% accuracy is bad. A detector that drops to 70% accuracy while still claiming 95% confidence is dangerous. ECE captures this distinction that accuracy alone misses.

3. **Add a new evaluation dimension.** Previous generalisation studies (Wang et al., 2020; Corvi et al., 2023) only measured accuracy and AUC. Adding ECE shows whether detectors fail safely or dangerously on new generators.

## 6. Research Gap and What This Project Does Differently

### 6.1 Summary of Prior Work

| Study | Detector | Generators Tested | Explainability | Calibration | Tests Across Generations |
|-------|----------|-------------------|----------------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Wang et al. (2020) | ResNet-50 | 12 GAN architectures | None | None | No (all GANs) |
| Bird & Lotfi (2024) | CNN/DenseNet | Stable Diffusion v1.4 only | LIME | None | No (single model) |
| Gragnaniello et al. (2021) | Various CNNs | Multiple GANs | Spectral analysis | None | No (all GANs) |
| Corvi et al. (2023) | Various | GANs + Diffusion | t-SNE | None | Partial (2 families) |
| Rossler et al. (2019) | XceptionNet | Face manipulation methods | None | None | No (manipulation only) |
| **This project** | **EfficientNet-B3** | **StyleGAN → SD → MJ v6 → GPT-4o** | **Grad-CAM** | **ECE + temp. scaling** | **Yes (4 generations)** |

### 6.2 What This Project Does That Has Not Been Done Before

No existing study combines all of the following:

1. **Testing across four chronologically distinct generator families** (GAN → diffusion → proprietary diffusion → autoregressive). Prior work tested at most two (Corvi et al.: GANs and diffusion). No published study has included GPT-4o or Midjourney v6 in a systematic detection evaluation.

2. **Measuring calibration alongside accuracy.** No prior detection study reports ECE. This means we currently have no evidence on whether detectors fail gracefully (low confidence when wrong) or catastrophically (high confidence when wrong) on new generators.

3. **Using Grad-CAM comparatively across generator families.** Bird and Lotfi (2024) applied LIME to one generator's outputs. No work has compared what a detector looks at across multiple generator families to visually explain why it succeeds on some and fails on others.

4. **Focusing on measuring the problem rather than proposing a solution.** Most papers in this area propose new, better detectors. This project deliberately trains a standard detector and then rigorously measures where and why it breaks — providing the foundational understanding needed before better solutions can be designed.

### 6.3 Expected Outcomes

This project will produce:

- **Quantitative results:** Accuracy, AUC, and ECE across images from StyleGAN, Stable Diffusion (in-distribution), Midjourney v6, and GPT-4o — showing progressive performance degradation as generators become more advanced.
- **Visual explanations:** Grad-CAM heatmaps comparing what the detector attends to on correct vs. incorrect predictions across different generators — showing which features it learned and where they disappear.
- **Calibration analysis:** Reliability diagrams and ECE scores showing whether the detector signals uncertainty on out-of-distribution images or makes confident errors — establishing the risk profile of current approaches.

Together, these answer the research question: "How well do detectors trained on current benchmarks generalise to next-generation models, and what accounts for the gap?"
